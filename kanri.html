Introduction:  12-Step Process (will work on any project)

  1. １：  Define (clearly what) the project (is)

    2. Planning:

  2. ２：  List all of the tasks
      (from the raw material for the rest of the planning-process)
  3. ３：  Plan the running order
    1. Put tasks in correct order
    2. Can use Post-It notes
    3. Construct a critical-path diagramme
  4. ４：  Add contingency
    1. Add a margin of safety to the plan
  5. ５：  Consider crashing
    1. What you can do if the total time comes out as being too long to be acceptable
    2. You are taking steps to condense time if the projected time for the project is longer than acceptable
    3. ('crashing a project, or condensing the time required for completion, is a process of identifying ways that you can speed up the project')
  6. ６：  Make a Gantt chart
  7. ７：  Calculate resource requirements
    1. Ascertain whether you have sufficient men人数 to do the project in the manner in which you wish to undertake it
  8. ８：  Assess risks & prepare plans of action

    9. Action:

  9. ９：  Monitor progress using the Gantt chart
  10. １０：  Monitor costs
  11. １１：  Re-adjust your plan
    1. . . . . if necessary
    2. will need to admiter either to boss or customer that need add'l time, money, or both
  12. １２：  Review

Step 1:  Define Your Project

企画とは？

  1. What defines a Project?:
    1. Finish date
      1. Projects finish
      2. Processes, 工場や病院等で行う活動、 continue perpetually
    2. Something new
      1. Involves doing s/t new, to wit, s/t that not done before
      2. Either never done it or do it so rarely that have forgotten how to do it
      3. Difficult & complicated ∴ requires plan
      4. CompareMoving for the 1st time ever or 1st time in a while w/ moving every 6 months due to being a soldier.  The latter is a process:  know what to do.

Define the Iron Triangle

  1. Seeting out to deliver s/t new by promised date
  2. Quality, Cost, & Time = 3 constrains . . . form iron triangle:
    1. Can only really pick 2
    2. Yet bosses & customers always want all 3
    3. ∴ need to ascertain:
      1. What obligee wants
      2. By when does he want it
      3. How much he can pay
  3. Always start by ascertaining what obligee (customer, boss, &c.) wants in terms of cost, quality, & time

Plan before You Commit

  1. Ascertain whether you can deliver project before 'committing' (saying, 'yes')
  2. Once you accept, the buck stops w/ you
  3. Bluntly stating that you can not achieve task sounds engative ∴ best to have plan that can 'waive around at' obligee
  4. Gantt charts = great charts to use
    1. Easy to understand
    2. Allows obligor (you, as project manager) to explain wherefore project has high cost or long time ∵ shows everything that a project entails
  5. Never say, "maybe', ∵ always = interpreted as a 'yes'

Kick-off Meetings

  1. Get app parties involved in a meeting ('kick-off meeting') in order to agree upon what project will look like
    1. Gain agreement on a single definition amongst multiple stakeholders, who will have different visions, as to what all parties want the project to look like
  2. Project manager can agree to do whatever obligee desires, but all parties must agree upon what project is going to be (can not work out details later)
    1. Can not start until plan project
    2. Can not plan project until know what obligee wants (to wit, what the project is)
    3. ∴ need that 'kick-off meeting'
    4. Otherwise, will cost more due to change sin what is req'd & resulting wasted work
  3. Ideally have 2:
    1. 1st :
      1. Discuss what parties want, time involved, cost involved, &c..
    2. Then, step away, & plan
    3. 2nd :
      1. Meeting to decide whether plan is feasible
      2. 2 outcomes場合は二つ有り:
        1. Good News:  agree & commit to plan
        2. Bad News:  explain that can not deliver project unless changes = made

Key Drivers of Project

  1. Determining which of 3 iron circles is most imp.
  2. Which of 3 iron circles drives project
  3. 例：
    1. W/ Olympics:  key driver = time
    2. W/ nuclear power-plant, quality
      1. No matter cost or time involved, needs to be of proper quality
  4. 3 Questions to Determine Key Driver of Project:
    1. Why that particular budget or time-limit?
      1. If no particular reason, then likely not a key driver
    2. What if?
      1. 例：  What if the wedding cake were a day late?
        1. Allow it late?  不可、当たり前
        2. Throw more money (以下、「￥￥」という) at it in order to ↑ speed?
        3. Sacrifice quality in order to ↑ speed?
    3. Can we trade?
      1. 'If you spent just a little more money, we could add some very nifty features'
        1. If customer responds, 'oh, such as?', then you know that he has more ¥¥
        2. If customer responds that there is no point in showing anyting more ∵ no add'l ¥¥, then know that ¥¥ is the limiting factor, or the key driver

Step 2:  List the Tasks

3 Steps to Listing All Project Tasks

  1. 概要：
    1. Hold a brain-storming meeting
    2. Categorise tasks ('W. B. S.')
    3. Ask others (incl. research)
  2. 詳ｼｮｳ細ｻｲ
    1. Hold a Brain-Storming Meeting
      1. Random list of everything of which can think that will be needed
      2. Sort out whether truly needed later
    2. Categorise Tasks (W. B. S. [Work Break-Down Structure])
      1. Organise tasks into categories
      2. 例： if were going to build a house, then would have structure (comprising the foundation, the walls, & the roof), the room (comprising the 台所や便所等), the services (comprising plumbing, electricity, &c.), &c.
      3. It is a tree of the tasks that enables you to identify anything that you may have missed during brain-storming
      4. It is a 'Work Break-Down Structure' (W. B. S.)
        1. It is a structured break-down of the work
    3. Ask Others
      1. Ask experts, either inside or outside of your organisation
      2. Could entail conducting research, consultants, internal experts, or men in other parts of org. that have done similar projects previously.
    4. Using steps not only ensures that not missing any tasks (& if miss tasks, could ↑ 費用
      5. más tarde) but also facilitates organising tasks in advance by 範疇

Choose the Level of Granularity

  1. If break down into details too minute, then plan → unwieldy & too complicated
  2. But ust break down details sufficiently, otherwise will not comprehend what each task comprises | will not be able to estimate the time & the cost req'd
  3. Signs that Tasks Are Too Big
    1. Unable to estimate time or cost req'd
      1. 例：  difficult to determine how long will take or how much will cost to install an entire kitchen.  Will need to break it down further, altho' not to the level of 'drill a hole through the wall' or 'push wire through hole'
      2. Tasks overlap
        1. 例：
          1. If were going to dig a 10-km. trench & then put in the pipes, you could list it as 'dig trench' followed by 'put in pipes'.
          2. However, in reality, you probably would not need to wait until the whole trench 完了 before putting in the pipes
          3. %therefore; need to decide when can start to install the pipes, such as after completing 1st kilometre of it
          4. Need to granulate the trench-task to show when you can start laying the pipes
      3. A task being 'on-going'
        1. A sure sign that you have not broken down a task sufficiently in order to estimate it
        2. If you list a project as 'on-going', it does not clearly define the time-line for completion, so obligor likely will cram all of work in towards of project . . . まーちょっとやでな、、僕にも有った笑汗笑
        3. Need to break it down weekly or by some other unit so that can monitor project to ensure remain 'on task'
      4. 要点
        1. Break tasks down until you can estimate their cost & the amount of time that they will require

    Step 3:  Plan the 'Running Order'

    1. Ascertain how the tasks 'fit together' | determine sequential order
    2. Point at which decide how you will actually undertake the project
    3. Can use Post-It Notes to organise, with steps that are interdependent & steps that may be completed independently & w/ relationships being represented by arrows ('the arrows mean "this task comes next"')
